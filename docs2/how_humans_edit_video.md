# How Professional Human Video Editors Work

Complete reference for building an AI video editor that thinks like a human. Covers workflow, mental models, craft techniques, engagement psychology, and platform strategy.

---

## 1. The Complete Editing Workflow

### Six Stages from Raw Footage to Final Output

**Stage 1: Ingest and Organization (~10-15% of time)**
- Import, transcode, create proxies, back up originals
- Log every clip: metadata, scene/take numbers, usability ratings
- Organize into bins by scene, angle, or content type
- Mark in/out points for every usable clip
- Rate footage (stars, colors, favorites)

**Stage 2: Paper Edit / String-Out (~10-15%)**
- Review transcripts, identify quotes/moments, lay out story on paper BEFORE touching timeline
- String-out: assemble best footage in sequence to see all options
- Selects reels: curated timelines of only the best material
- Pairs down raw footage into manageable working material

**Stage 3: Assembly Cut (~10%)**
- All footage roughly assembled in story order
- Runs 2-3x final length, imprecise, loose pacing
- Purpose: verify all needed material exists

**Stage 4: Rough Cut (~30-40% -- the hardest phase)**
- Trim excess, block major scenes sequentially
- Select between competing takes
- Establish narrative flow and pacing
- Incorporate temp music/SFX
- This is where the most intensive editorial work happens

**Stage 5: Fine Cut (~15-20%)**
- Frame-level decisions on every shot and cut
- Finesse transitions, text, graphics
- Catch blemishes and continuity errors
- Ends at "Picture Lock" -- timing will not change after this

**Stage 6: Finishing (~10-15%)**
- Sound editing/mixing, color correction/grading, VFX, titles
- Saved for last so no time is wasted on clips that get cut

**Key principle:** The rough cut consumes disproportionate time because it involves the hardest creative decisions.

## 2. How Editors Think

### First Watch: What They Look For
- **Emotional moments**: sparkle, sadness, joy, beauty -- "hard to describe but makes a film alive"
- **Performance peaks**: best delivery, most genuine reactions
- **Story beats**: moments that advance narrative or reveal character
- **Usability**: technical quality (focus, exposure, audio)
- **Cut points**: natural pauses, movements, eye blinks

**Critical insight:** Editors hold onto first impressions because "by the time you get the first rough cut those first impressions are gone and you can't get those back." Some make sticky notes during first viewing.

### The Selects Process
1. Watch every clip while noting strongest emotional/story moments
2. Rate on usability and emotional impact
3. Mark in/out points for best segments
4. Create selects reels of only the best material
5. Organize by theme, scene, or character

### Finding the Edit
Walter Murch: deciding three things over and over -- **"What shot shall I use? Where shall I begin it? Where shall I end it?"** An average film has ~1,000 edits = ~3,000 decisions.

### Editing is Subtraction
Hollywood shoots ~100 hours for a 2-hour film (~98% removed). The hardest part: removing excellent footage that doesn't serve the whole. "Beloved scenes may be beautifully photographed, but if it slows the rhythms, distracts from the emotional line, or confuses the narrative, it simply must go."

### The Gut Feeling
An editor's "gut" is **pattern recognition from experience**. Academic research describes it as an "embodied experience" -- physiological and cognitive response to film material becomes a rhythmic storytelling instinct.

Thelma Schoonmaker stays off set deliberately for "cold perspective" -- works with footage alone without memories of being there.

## 3. The Art of the Cut

### Walter Murch's Rule of Six

| Priority | Criterion | Weight | Description |
|----------|-----------|--------|-------------|
| 1 | **Emotion** | 51% | Does the cut feel right emotionally? |
| 2 | **Story** | 23% | Does it advance the story? |
| 3 | **Rhythm** | 10% | Is it at a rhythmically interesting moment? |
| 4 | **Eye-trace** | 7% | Does it respect where the audience is looking? |
| 5 | **2D plane** | 5% | Does it respect screen geography? |
| 6 | **3D space** | 4% | Does it respect spatial continuity? |

**"Sacrifice upward, never downward."** A cut that nails emotion but breaks continuity is still good. A technically perfect cut that feels emotionally dead is a failure.

### Cut Types

| Type | Technique | When to Use |
|------|-----------|-------------|
| **J-Cut** | Next scene's audio starts before visual | Opening scenes, foreshadowing, urgency |
| **L-Cut** | Current audio continues over next visual | Dialogue, reaction shots, natural flow |
| **Match Cut** | Compositionally/thematically similar shots | Time passage, symbolism, visual metaphor |
| **Cut on Action** | Cut mid-movement, complete in next shot | Action, interactions -- nearly invisible |
| **Jump Cut** | Visible cut within same framing | Vlogs (embrace), or hide with B-roll/zoom |

### The Kuleshov Effect
Viewers derive more meaning from juxtaposition of two shots than either shot alone. Same expressionless face + soup = hunger. Same face + coffin = grief. **Meaning is created in the cut, not in the shot.**

### Eye Trace
- Keep subject in roughly same screen area across cuts
- Brightness, color, focus highlight important areas
- Fast cuts make eyes dart; slow cuts let them settle
- If subject moves to completely different screen position, it pulls viewers out

### The Blink Theory
Murch discovered natural blinks align with natural cut points. A person blinks at every new thought or emotion. Film editors should cut where the audience would blink. Not every blink is a cut, but blinks are built-in edit points.

### Invisible vs Visible Editing
- **Invisible (continuity)**: Classical Hollywood -- establishing shots, shot/reverse, 180-degree rule. Viewer focuses on story, not editing
- **Visible (discontinuous)**: Breaking rules deliberately -- jump cuts, unconventional transitions. For artistic effect or disorientation

## 4. Pacing and Rhythm

Murch: **"Editing is certainly 70% about rhythm"** -- like telling a joke, wrong rhythm kills it.

### Fast vs Slow Cuts

| Pace | Effect | When |
|------|--------|------|
| Fast (<2s) | Energy, urgency, chaos | Action, climaxes |
| Medium (2-5s) | Engagement, momentum | Standard narrative |
| Slow (5s+) | Reflection, tension, dread | Emotional moments, horror |

### Breathing Room
Intentional pauses for audience to digest. **"When everything breathes equally, it takes away the effect of staying in a moment."**

### Music-Driven Editing
- Sync cuts to rhythmic pulse of score
- Slow melodies = longer shots; fast tracks = rapid cuts
- Beat matching creates seamless audio-visual rhythm

### Building to Climax
1. Start with longer, lingering shots (normalcy)
2. Progressively shorten shot duration approaching climax
3. Oscillate fast/slow for psychological peaks and valleys
4. Cross-cut storylines during climax for suspense
5. Balance tension with release to avoid fatigue

### Average Shot Length Data

| Context | ASL |
|---------|-----|
| Pre-1960 Hollywood | 8-11s |
| 2000s Hollywood | <2s |
| Action films | ~2s (~3000+ shots) |
| Average film | 4-6s (~1250 shots) |
| Romance/drama | 6-10s+ |

## 5. Short-Form / Social Media Editing

### How Short-Form Differs from Film
- Viewers decide in **2-3 seconds** to stay or swipe (vs 30s in film)
- Effective engagement window: **5.3-7.8 seconds**
- 85% of views happen without sound
- Every frame is a potential thumbnail

### Hook-Value-CTA Framework

| Phase | Timing | Purpose |
|-------|--------|---------|
| **Hook** | 0-3s | Pattern interrupt, bold visual, compelling question |
| **Value** | 3-15s | Core message, promise delivery |
| **CTA** | Last 5s | Comment, follow, click |

Hooks exploit: **pattern interruption, curiosity gaps, social proof.**

### Retention Techniques
- New visual every 1.5-3s during escalation
- First cut within 3 seconds
- Static shots >5s hurt retention
- Rapid cuts every 0.5-1s = 3.3x higher completion on Reels
- Open loops (unfinished thoughts) keep viewers watching

### Silent-First Editing
- Build story visually assuming no one hears it, then enhance with sound
- Text overlays boost engagement 25%, retention 30%, view time 28%
- Test in silence before publishing -- if you can't understand it, revise

### Optimal Lengths

| Platform | Sweet Spot |
|----------|-----------|
| TikTok | 15-30s (21-34s range leads) |
| Reels | 15-30s |
| YouTube Shorts | 30-60s |
| YouTube long-form | 8-15 min |

## 6. Screen Recording / Tutorial Editing

### Zooming into UI
- Auto-zoom on cursor/click areas
- Use sparingly -- too much is distracting
- Quick zoom to direct attention, hold 2-3s
- Reduce webcam overlay during zooms

### Removing Dead Time
- Cut navigation, loading, typing pauses
- Speed up boring sections rather than cutting (preserves context)
- Remove audio stumbles and retakes

### Making Code Readable on Mobile
- fontSize 14+, lineHeight 22+, high-contrast themes
- Record at 1280x720 or 1920x1080
- Zoom into specific code sections rather than showing entire screen

### Webcam Overlay
- Customize shape, size, position
- Reduce size during zooms
- Position in corner that won't obstruct content

## 7. Audio Editing for Video

### Audio Layering (bottom to top)
1. **Ambiance**: Environment, room tone
2. **Dialogue/Narration**: Cleaned and normalized
3. **Music**: Background score, matched to tone
4. **Sound Effects**: Transitions, impacts, Foley
5. **Final Mix**: Balance all layers

### Music Psychology
Music bypasses logic, goes straight to limbic system (emotions, memory). A single piece can completely change a scene's tone. Timing is critical -- well-timed cue enhances; mistimed cue disrupts.

### LUFS Standards

| Platform | Target |
|----------|--------|
| YouTube | -14 LUFS |
| Streaming | -14 to -16 LUFS |
| US TV (ATSC) | -24 LUFS |
| EU TV (EBU R128) | -23 LUFS |

### The Radio Edit Test
Test video as audio-only. If story/emotion works without visuals, audio is solid. Then test without audio for visual story. Then combine.

### SFX Usage

| Effect | When |
|--------|------|
| Whoosh | Scene transitions, quick movements |
| Impact | Key moments, hard cuts, reveals |
| Pop/Ding | Text appearing, callouts |
| Ambiance | Scene establishment |

## 8. Color and Visual Polish

### Correction vs Grading

| | Color Correction | Color Grading |
|--|-----------------|---------------|
| Purpose | Fix -- "does this look real?" | Style -- "does this fit the story?" |
| Order | Always first | Always second |
| Goal | True-to-life colors | Emotional impact |

### Color and Mood
- Warm (orange/yellow): comfort, nostalgia
- Cool (blue/teal): isolation, technology
- High contrast: drama, energy
- Low contrast: dreamy, vintage
- Desaturation: bleakness, tension

### Mobile Viewing
- Higher contrast essential (variable lighting conditions)
- Bold color choices read better on small screens
- Test on actual mobile devices

## 9. Engagement Psychology

### Attention Data
- Average engagement window: **7.8 seconds** (2026)
- Short-form disengagement: **5.3 seconds** without strong hook
- YouTube algorithm prioritizes >60% retention in first 8 seconds

### Serial Position Effect
- **Primacy**: First items remembered best
- **Recency**: Last items remembered best
- **Application**: Front-load and back-load key messages. Least critical content in middle.

### Curiosity Gap
Specific curiosity arises from discrepancy between what viewer knows and wants to know. Tease outcomes without revealing them. Ask questions at start, answer at end.

### Zeigarnik Effect
Unfinished tasks stick in memory. Open loops maintain engagement. Don't resolve everything at once -- string out revelations.

### Pattern Interrupts
Break visual monotony with sudden changes. Trigger dopamine release through reward system activation. Types: zoom shifts, angle changes, color changes, unexpected audio, text at unpredictable intervals.

## 10. Problem Solving

### Fixing Broken Footage

| Problem | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| Shaky video | Warp stabilization, crop in for room, slow-motion |
| Bad audio | Low pass filters, room tone layering, music masking |
| Wrong framing | Crop/reposition, scale up, overlays to mask |
| Bad exposure | Color correction, vignettes, grading |

### Jump Cut Solutions

| Approach | Technique | Context |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| Hide | B-roll overlay | Professional/corporate |
| Hide | Zoom in/out | YouTube talking head |
| Embrace | Visible jump | Vlogs, energetic content |
| Smooth | Morph cut | Interviews |

### Common Beginner Mistakes
1. Poor audio quality
2. Overusing flashy transitions
3. Shots too long
4. Messy timeline (no organization)
5. Inconsistent typography
6. Over-editing (too many effects)
7. Ignoring audio levels
8. No organization before editing
9. Cutting without intention
10. Not testing on target device

## 11. Professional Editor Insights

### Walter Murch
- "What shot? Where begin it? Where end it?" -- the 3 decisions, made 1000+ times
- Editing is "psychological practice with a goal of anticipating and controlling audience thoughts"
- Blink theory: natural blinks align with natural cut points
- "Always try to do the most with the least. Suggestion is always more effective than exposition."
- Cutting away before character finishes = words taken at face value. Lingering = audience judges from eyes.
- "Not to overdo it. It frequently takes more work to decide where NOT to cut."

### Thelma Schoonmaker
- Key qualities: "strong sense of discipline and a musical sense, and ability to collaborate"
- "The priority is absolutely on the best take for performance" -- accepts continuity errors
- "You never know what happens when you put two pieces of film together. Sometimes an accidental thing will happen and we will be thrilled."
- Stays off set for cold perspective

### What Separates Good from Great
A good editor follows rules and delivers clean work. A great editor:
- Has narrative instincts -- feels when a story works
- Reads micro-expressions to find emotional truth
- Creates meaning through juxtaposition
- Knows when to break rules for impact
- Can restructure an entire piece when the approach fails
- Manages rhythm at micro (frame) and macro (sequence) levels
- Treats editing as rewriting -- the final draft

## 12. The Editor's Decision Framework

### For Any Moment, An Editor Asks:

1. **Does this serve the emotion?** (51%) -- What should the audience feel?
2. **Does this advance the story?** (23%) -- Is this redundant?
3. **Does this fit the rhythm?** (10%) -- Building appropriately?
4. **Does this guide the eye?** (7%) -- Where is the audience looking?
5. **Does this maintain visual grammar?** (5%) -- Screen direction consistent?
6. **Does this maintain spatial continuity?** (4%) -- 180-degree rule?

### When Goals Conflict

| Conflict | Resolution |
|----------|------------|
| Performance vs continuity | Performance wins (Schoonmaker) |
| Pacing vs information | Cut info; add back only if story suffers |
| Music sync vs story beat | Story wins; adjust music |
| Visual beauty vs emotional truth | Emotional truth wins |
| Retention vs artistic vision | Platform-dependent |

### Questions Before Every Cut
- What is the audience thinking right now?
- What do I want them to think next?
- What is the minimum needed to communicate this?
- Does removing this hurt story or help rhythm?
- Is there a more interesting way to convey this?

### Murch's Audience Feedback Principle
"Listen to the emotions of the audience, but not necessarily the specific feedback." Audiences detect when something is wrong but cannot diagnose what. The editor translates discomfort into specific fixes.

## 13. What Humans Do Without Realizing It

These are the implicit skills and knowledge that human editors apply unconsciously. An AI must be explicitly prompted or programmed for each one because it cannot learn these through general experience.

### Emotional Reading of Faces
Humans instantly read micro-expressions (a slight eyebrow raise, lip tension, eye widening) and know which take has the most authentic emotion. We do this in milliseconds without conscious analysis. An AI needs explicit instruction: "choose the take where the speaker's expression matches the emotional weight of what they're saying."

### Breathing and Speech Rhythm
Editors unconsciously feel where a speaker takes a breath, where their voice rises or falls, where emphasis lands. They cut on exhales, never on inhales. They feel the "weight" of a pause -- whether it's dramatic (keep) or dead air (cut). AI needs: explicit breath detection, speech prosody analysis, and rules about which pauses are intentional.

### Visual Weight and Balance
Humans intuitively sense when a frame is "heavy" on one side or feels unbalanced. An editor adjusts framing without thinking about the rule of thirds -- they just feel it. AI needs: explicit composition analysis, center-of-mass calculation for visual elements, and rules about visual balance.

### Contextual Relevance of Screen Content
When a speaker says "look at this dashboard," a human editor instantly knows WHICH part of the screen is "this." They track the speaker's gaze, mouse cursor, and verbal references simultaneously. AI needs: explicit correlation between transcript keywords and on-screen elements via OCR/object detection.

### The "It Feels Off" Detector
Editors have a general sense when something is wrong -- a cut feels jarring, audio doesn't match, pacing drags -- even before they can articulate why. This is pattern recognition from thousands of hours of watching content. AI needs: explicit quality metrics for every dimension (audio sync, visual continuity, pacing variance, eye-trace violation).

### Cultural and Social Context
A human editor knows that showing a product logo for too long feels like an ad, that certain gestures mean different things in different cultures, that a particular music style evokes a specific era. They adjust tone for LinkedIn vs TikTok without being told the audience difference. AI needs: explicit platform personality profiles and cultural context rules.

### Narrative Causality
Humans naturally understand that if someone says "I'm going to show you X" then X must follow. We track promises and payoffs unconsciously across minutes of content. AI needs: explicit promise/payoff tracking with open-loop detection (ClipCannon's `get_narrative_flow` does this).

### Audio-Visual Gestalt
Humans perceive audio and video as a unified experience. A slightly off-sync audio track "feels wrong" even if we can't identify why. We perceive the emotional tone of music + visuals as one thing, not two separate streams. AI needs: explicit A/V sync verification, mood-matching between audio characteristics and visual content.

### Anticipation of Viewer Behavior
An experienced editor watches their own cut and simultaneously simulates a first-time viewer's experience. They predict where attention will wander, where someone might reach for their phone, where a viewer would laugh or lean in. AI needs: explicit attention modeling based on content type, pacing, and visual complexity.

### The "Leave Them Wanting More" Instinct
Humans naturally know when something has been shown long enough. We feel the moment when a shot overstays its welcome or when cutting away a moment early creates desire to see more. This is the difference between a 3-second hold (perfect) and a 4-second hold (one second too long). AI needs: explicit shot duration rules calibrated by content type and platform.

### Physical Empathy with the Speaker
Editors often unconsciously mirror the speaker's body language while cutting. They feel the speaker's energy -- leaning forward (engaged), leaning back (relaxed), gesturing (emphasis). This physical empathy guides pacing decisions. AI needs: body language detection and mapping to energy/emphasis scores.

### Sound Design Intuition
A human knows that a "whoosh" belongs on a fast transition but not a slow dissolve. That an "impact" sound enhances a text pop but would be jarring on a face cut. That silence before a reveal builds more tension than a riser. AI needs: explicit SFX-to-transition mapping rules and context-aware sound placement logic.

### The Difference Between "Polished" and "Over-Produced"
Humans intuitively feel when editing crosses from professional to fake. Too many effects, too much color grading, too many transitions -- the video starts feeling like a template rather than authentic content. This line is cultural and shifts with trends. AI needs: explicit effect density limits per platform and content type.

### Temporal Context Awareness
Editors maintain a mental model of where they are in the video at all times. They know "we're 40% through, the energy should be building" or "we're near the end, time to bring it home." Every decision is colored by position in the timeline. AI needs: explicit timeline position awareness and pacing curves that vary by video phase (intro/middle/climax/outro).

### Implicit Grammar of Visual Storytelling
There are hundreds of unwritten rules: you don't cut to a wider shot when tension is building. You don't use warm colors for sad moments. You don't speed up emotional dialogue. You match the energy of a cut to the energy of the content. None of these are written down -- they're absorbed through years of watching and creating. AI needs: these rules made explicit as constraints in the editing decision engine.

## 14. The 10 Commandments of Editing

1. **Emotion is king** -- every decision serves how the audience should feel
2. **Every cut needs a reason** -- never cut without purpose
3. **Less is more** -- suggestion beats exposition
4. **Rhythm is musical** -- editing has tempo, beats, rests, crescendos
5. **Guide the eye** -- manage visual attention across every transition
6. **Performance trumps continuity** -- choose the better take
7. **First impressions are irreplaceable** -- initial emotional response is sacred data
8. **Meaning lives in juxtaposition** -- the cut creates the story
9. **Audio is half the experience** -- bad audio kills good video
10. **Know your platform** -- 2s hooks for TikTok, 8s for YouTube, patience for film

## 15. Decision Flowchart

```
Raw moment arrives -->
  1. Is there emotion here? (if no, consider cutting entirely)
  2. Does it advance the story? (if no, is emotion strong enough alone?)
  3. Where does it fit in the rhythm? (pacing context)
  4. What is the audience looking at right before? (eye trace)
  5. How long should it hold? (platform + content type)
  6. What comes after? (transition planning)
  7. Does the audio support or fight the visual? (sound design)
```
